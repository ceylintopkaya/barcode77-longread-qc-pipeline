I performed QC analysis of the barcode77 long-read sequencing dataset generated using Oxford Nanopore technology. To automate the analysis process, I developed a Snakemake-based pipeline and managed the computational environment using Conda.

In the first step, I analyzed the raw sequencing data using Python. For each read, I calculated read length, GC content, and quality score. To summarize the dataset, I computed mean, median, minimum, maximum, and standard deviation values, as well as N50, Q10/Q20/Q30 quality thresholds, and coverage estimation. All results were organized into a detailed Excel report.

For the visualization stage, I used both Python and R to better interpret the data.

Using Python, I created the following visualizations:

Log-scale histograms to visualize the wide read length distribution ranging from 86 bp to 686 kb.

A hexbin density plot to examine the relationship between read length and quality scores across more than 81,000 reads, identifying the length ranges where the highest-quality reads cluster.

Using R, I created the following visualizations:

Density plots to analyze the statistical distribution patterns of the data.

Violin and boxplots to examine the distribution width (variance) of quality scores and GC content. These plots clearly reveal the overall homogeneity of the dataset and the proportion of low-quality outlier reads.

Key Findings

Read Count and Length:

A total of 81,011 reads were analyzed, with a total yield of 84.11 Mb. The mean read length was 1,038 bp, and the N50 value was 1,761 bp. The shortest read was 86 bp, while the longest read reached 686,155 bp. This ultra-long read is a significant feature that can improve genome assembly quality by spanning repetitive genomic regions.

Quality Score:

The mean quality score was Q17.9 (~98.4% accuracy). The distribution according to quality thresholds is as follows:

Q10 and above: 77.8% (usable for most analyses)

Q20 and above: 41.2% (suitable for variant calling)

Q30 and above: 8.8% (suitable for high-accuracy applications)

GC Content:

The mean GC content was 53.0%, which is consistent with the expected GC range for bacterial genomes (~52%). The relatively narrow distribution (standard deviation 4.7%) indicates no significant contamination or GC bias in the dataset.

Coverage:

With a total yield of 84.11 Mb, the estimated coverage for a typical 5 Mb bacterial genome is approximately 17X.

Conclusion and Recommendation

Overall, the visualizations and statistical results indicate no major technical issues in the dataset. Additionally, the presence of a 686 kb ultra-long read provides a valuable advantage for downstream analyses.

Based on these QC results, the dataset appears suitable for further analyses such as reference-based alignment, de novo genome assembly, or structural variant analysis.
